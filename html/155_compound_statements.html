Compound statements
Compound statements contain (groups of) other statements
They affect or control the execution of those other statements in some way.
In general, compound statements span multiple lines, although in simple incarnations a whole compound statement may be contained in one line.
if, while, for (traditional control flow constructs), try, while, with are all examples
Function and class definitions are also syntactically compound statements.
Formal Definition
A compound statement consists of one or more clauses.
clause
A clause consists of a header and suite.
The clause headers of a particular compound statement are all at the same indentation level.
Each clause header begins with a uniquely identifying keyword and ends with a colon.
suite
A suite is a group of statements controlled by a clause.
A suite can be one or more semicolon-separated simple statements on the same line as the header, following the header’s colon.
Or a suite can be one or more indented statements on subsequent lines.
Only the latter form of a suite can contain nested compound statements;
Note that the semicolon binds tighter than the colon in this context, so that in the following example, either all or none of the print() calls are executed 
  if x < y < z: print(x); print(y); print(z)
